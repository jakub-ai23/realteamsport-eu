How I started a sports club as an immigrant in Munich

I moved to Munich in 2019. I didn't know anyone. My German was functional — I could order food and navigate the U-Bahn — but not fluent. I had a roundnet net in my bag and a vague idea that I wanted to play.

The sport barely existed in Germany at the time. A few clubs in Berlin. Some scattered players in Hamburg. Munich: nothing. If I wanted to play, I had to find people and build something from scratch.

Starting a sports club in Germany requires paperwork I didn't know existed. A Vereinssatzung — the club statute, which must meet specific legal requirements. A founding meeting with at least seven people, who must all sign the document. A club bank account, which requires the club to be legally registered, which requires the bank account. Classic bureaucracy loop. The Finanzamt was its own adventure.

I didn't know any of this when I showed up at the Englischer Garten one Sunday morning with a net, a post on an expats Facebook group, and hope. Eight people came. Half had never heard of roundnet. Two had never played any sport competitively in their lives. One brought their dog.

We figured out the paperwork over the following months. A lawyer friend helped with the statute. The founding meeting happened in someone's living room. The bank account took six weeks and three visits to two different banks.

By year two, we had a regular training session every week, a WhatsApp group with 40 people, and a waiting list for our first beginner workshop. Players came from Austria, Czech Republic, the UK, France. The community built itself — I just kept showing up and making it easy to join.

What I learned: communities don't need infrastructure to start. They need one person who shows up consistently. The paperwork follows. The culture comes from the people. Your job is to create the conditions and then get out of the way.

The club is still running. I moved to Vienna in 2023, but the Munich chapter is in good hands.
