Diving in the Red Sea: what happens when you go too deep

I've been diving since 2019. Started in Croatia, moved to colder water in the Adriatic, eventually made it to the Red Sea twice. Egypt first, then back again.

The Red Sea is something else. Visibility of 30 meters on a good day. Walls that drop to 800 meters — you hover at 20 meters and look down into nothing, which is either terrifying or beautiful depending on your relationship with depth. The fish don't care about you at all, which is the best thing about diving: you're the visitor, and everything else is just living its life.

The "too deep" incident wasn't dramatic, which is part of the point. 28 meters, a current that had been pushing gently all dive and started pushing harder, and a moment where I realized I'd drifted further from the wall than I intended. Not in danger. But not where I was supposed to be, and moving in a direction I hadn't chosen.

The instinct is to fight it. Kick harder, go faster, get back to where you were. The right answer is the opposite: slow down, assess, signal the guide, ascend slightly to get above the worst of it. I knew this from the training. Doing it when your body is saying "do something" is a different skill.

I was fine. The guide was fine. We finished the dive. But I've thought about that moment more than most things that happened that week, because it's a clean example of something that applies everywhere: calm is not a personality trait. It's a practice. You build it in low-stakes situations so it's available in higher-stakes ones.

The Red Sea is still on my list. Dahab next time. More stories on the way.
